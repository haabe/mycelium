# Mycelium reached a non-developer user at the affective layer

**Audience**: evaluators asking whether Mycelium's value extends beyond developers; contributors curious whether the brief-synthesis mechanism works on non-software products.
**Time to read**: 3 min.
**Last updated**: 2026-05-20.

## The session

2026-05-20. Edith-Mari Pedersen Bartnes — first non-developer user to test Mycelium end-to-end — ran `/mycelium:start` on a book project (content_publication product-type). ~10-15 minutes. Reached the assumption-test stage. Setup: Håvard launched, she took the keyboard.

## What she said and what surfaced

Reading the brief Mycelium produced from her own input, she nearly cried. Håvard's verbatim observation: *"Even though it was her own words, it was captured and presented well."*

Completing the assumption test, she described it as **very valuable** and said she felt the agent and the system **really saw her** and what she was trying to achieve with her book project.

A small note before that mattered: she was nervous in a way she couldn't quite name, until she realised she was talking to an AI rather than a person. The pressure dropped. The same warmth in the brief that produced the recognition moment had been producing social-presence pressure before the AI-ness clicked. One property, two effects.

## What this case is evidence of

- **Brief-synthesis-as-identity-mirror works for non-developer users on non-software products.** The mechanism that reflects a user's own framing back to them in structured form can produce affective recognition, not just cognitive validation. For a personal-investment project (her own book), this matters more than any rational utility argument.
- **Assumption-test mechanism produces "really seen" feedback on content_publication.** Previous dogfood was almost entirely software/framework products; this is the first concrete validation that the L2 assumption-test surface holds at the affective layer for content work.
- **Time-to-recognition is short.** 10-15 minutes on `/mycelium:start` reached the emotional payoff. Not days; not multiple sessions.

## What this case is NOT evidence of

- That Mycelium is friction-free. Five friction items surfaced in the same session (repo bootstrap, Claude Code save-prompts, long save-structure output confusing for non-tech-savvy readers, chat scroll auto-bounce, "You are here" wayfinding gap at the assumption-test → deep-dive-interview transition). One graduation candidate logged in corrections.md.
- That Mycelium reaches non-dev users at scale. Sample size 1. Specific relationship (founder's wife). Real signal but not statistical.
- That every content_publication project will land the same way. Edith-Mari is a particular person with a particular book project; the brief-synthesis recognition moment depended on the framework reading her own framing back accurately.

## What followed

The "You are here" wayfinding gap at the assumption-test → deep-dive-interview transition graduated into a corrections.md entry (2026-05-20). Previous orientation-mechanism work covered initial flow; this is a recurrence at a new stage. The correction extends the wayfinding surface to fire at every phase transition, with the explicit rationale that non-developer users need orientation more than developers do — they don't carry mental models of agent flows by default.
